Is Disobedience Good?

Posted on May 24, 2011 by Emotional Muscle

Recently, a very popular mother’s blogsite posted an article extolling disobedience. The author advised that parents should support children in whatever they want, so they wouldn’t grow up to be unhappy and repressed. The blog author implied that the alternative to complete laissez-faire is rigid authoritarian suppression of a child’s spirit.
Many parents who responded felt uncomfortable with this idea of encouraging disobedience, especially when it involved issues of the child’s safety. Some wondered what message we were giving to our kids and whether there really was a conflict between following necessary safety or family rules and having initiative and joy.

In our experience, children with no limits usually become anxious, entitled and aggressive. A major emotional achievement of early childhood is the acceptance of realistic power and limitations. This is an emotional muscle that needs a lot of work and practice. Finding pleasure in real capacities and positive effectiveness in the world is a resource for life. One of our basic assumptions is that joy and strength can be found in the pleasures of reality, but to find them everyone has also to accept the limits of reality.

Parents can promote the growth of this muscle by being explicit about what your child is in charge of, what parents are in charge of, and what nobody is in charge of. As early as two years old, a major challenge for parents and children is the battle over control. “Me do it!” is the anthem of toddlers. We take up this issue in various ways in our book, one being the game of the “three buckets.”

Nora was ordering everyone around about what she wanted to do and what she wanted them to do. She seemed desperately invested in being the boss. Her parents were frustrated and felt helpless and angry. They didn’t want to squash her assertion or initiative, but her exasperated parents likened her to a dictator. In the context of the continuing discussion about who is in charge of what, the family consultant at their school devised an activity for them to do with Nora and her older sister Katie.

They set up three buckets and threw balls of paper into them. One bucket was for what Nora and Katie were in charge of, one was for what their parents or teachers were in charge of, and the last was for what no one can be in charge of. Each person shouted something out in turn and decided, with the help of the others, which bucket to toss the ball into.

For instance, when Nora shouted “bedtime,” her parents said, “Bedtime goes into our bucket, but sleep time goes into your bucket because you are in charge of your own body.” When Katie shouted, “Sunshine,” Nora, nearly three, said, “No one is in charge of that. It has to go in the last bucket.” Everyone cheered. Their daddy shouted, “Rain! I’m in charge of the rain!” The children laughed and contradicted him, insisting that his ball go in the last bucket too. They also were learning from his joke that everyone may sometimes wish to be in charge of everything. And so it went.

This activity could be repeated over time since, as the children grew, there were changes in what they could be in charge of. One day, Nora said, “Let’s play the buckets. I want to put in there that I’m in charge of riding my trike and using the potty.” “Hurray” said everyone and they all enjoyed throwing in the balls for the new skills.

In this concrete enjoyable way parents can convey the wisdom of the ages, for all ages --
the willingness to do what you can do, the strength to accept what you can’t do and the wisdom to know the difference between the two.
