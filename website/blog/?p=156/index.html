Natural consequences

Posted on May 26, 2011 by Emotional Muscle

Dear Kerry,
What discipline strategies can I use when there isn’t a natural consequence for a behavior? My 6-year-old Sammy was testing limits the other day telling me that he was not going to go to school, then he started talking back to me, mocking “nnah nnah nnah.” I had no idea what to do—I obviously told him he’s being disrespectful and I don’t want to be around him if he speaks that way- but truly, that isn’t enough. And it makes me flipping mad when he is such a smug little jerk.
KI, Illinois

Dear KI,
When one’s own child is being so mean and disrespectful, the helpless, angry feeling is pretty hard to bear. It’s tough to resist the impulse to just fight back. Your question is a good effort to reach for your own strengths to help Sammy handle himself more constructively.

It sounds like you generally try to use the ‘natural consequences’ method. If he pours milk in his glass, and doesn’t stop himself soon enough, it will spill over and he will have to clean it up. Having to do the job before he can have his snack will help him remember to be more careful next time. Learning the cause and effect connections between actions and consequences teaches children to anticipate what might happen, instead of just punishing. This is the foundation of judgment, an important emotional muscle we want children to develop.

Sammy’s conscience, his “inside helper” that makes those judgments, that tells us what is wise to do and what isn’t, what will make us feel like a good person or not, was not available to him in that moment. Although he probably can usually access it to manage his behavior better, he couldn’t get it together that day to reach down inside and act his age. You don’t say why he didn’t want to go to school in the first place. He might have been covering up a worry with blustering. Worry or not, however, there is no excuse for behavior that is disrespectful. We can often explain naughtiness, but there is usually no justification for it.

How can we use your natural angry reaction to help Sammy learn a better way to deal with his feelings? When Sammy was disrespectful to you, I think you lost respect for him. Instead of your usual feeling that he is a nice boy, you thought he was being a jerk. That is the real natural consequence of his behavior.

Your dip in respect suggests a natural consequence. Lots of people might get impatient and say ‘just take his TV time away or say he can’t play with his new toy.’ I think they might be right, but for the wrong reasons. There are some important steps that have to come in between the bad behavior and your taking something away, if you want Sammy to grow inside from the experience.

When Sammy says “nyah, nyah,” you can tell him you are disappointed. “I thought you had already learned to manage your feelings better than that. 6-year-olds are strong enough to say what they want in words and not put other people down to feel strong. But you’re acting like your baby sister, Sarah, who doesn’t know how to do that yet.”

You might remind him that you think he actually does know how to pull himself together. If he still can’t manage, feed that back. “It looks like you aren’t able to do that for yourself today. You are going to need my help, just like Sarah sometimes does. That’s part of taking care of her and taking care of you.”

By this point Sammy might have stopped, intrigued or insulted at being likened to his baby sister. That’s not such a bad thing, since he shouldn’t feel good about what’s been going on. Your priority here isn’t to protect his self-esteem at all costs; he can stand a little discomfort about himself, especially if it makes him stop and think. One of your goals is to enlist his actual 6-year-old capacities and emotional muscles– his ability to pause and reflect, to imagine, to anticipate consequences, tolerate some frustration, use his words, trust your judgment and his own, etc.

If he keeps on, you can make the crucial connection that will be a building block for learning. “I don’t let Sarah do the wrong thing. I don’t let her play with your 6-year-old toys, because she doesn’t know how to stop herself putting them in her mouth. That’s just like the trouble you are having today stopping yourself using a mean voice with me. So I will put your new small Legos away in a box for a week, while you practice using your strong stopping muscles to control that mean voice. We’ll take them out and try again next Saturday.”

You will have taken away his toys, but for a different reason and with a different explanation for Sammy. Instead of just tit-for-tat, you are teaching him that his behavior has consequences that matter to him, and that you are willing to back up the learning with actions to help him manage his feelings and develop the emotional muscle he needs to feel good about himself.
